There's a robin that lives in the hedge at the end of the garden. I've named him Gerald, which the Admiral says is a mistake because now I'm invested and we leave in June.

Gerald is approximately the size of a tennis ball. He has the rust-red chest you'd expect, the round eyes, the general air of a small creature that has never once felt threatened in its life. Every morning when I go out to give Trixie her first walk, Gerald appears on the fence post, tilts his head, and watches us with the calm confidence of someone who owns the property.

British robins are a genuinely different animal from the robins I grew up with in Colorado. American robins are large, migratory, skittish — they're in your yard, then they're gone. The European robin is a sedentary bird and an intensely territorial one, and centuries of living alongside gardeners has made them behaviorally strange in a way I find completely delightful.

They associate humans with disturbed soil. When a gardener digs, worms come up, and the robin has learned that a human with a spade is basically a food delivery service. So they've evolved to be *comfortable* near us in a way that most small birds haven't. Gerald follows me around the garden when I'm weeding. He lands within arm's reach. He once hopped onto the edge of the raised bed while I was three feet away and just... looked at me.

I've been birding seriously for a few years now — started in the Bahamas, where the shorebirds were spectacular and I had nothing but time on the boat — but I've never had a bird look at me like it knew something I didn't.

We've ticked off a solid list here in Shropshire: jackdaws (endlessly entertaining, clearly intelligent), a pair of great tits in the oak by the gate, a nuthatch that I spotted once and then never again, and what I'm fairly confident was a treecreeper, though the light was bad and I couldn't get the binoculars up in time.

But it's Gerald I think about. He'll still be here after we've gone. New sitters will come and he'll assess them from the fence post, tilt his head, make his calculation. The garden will have a different human moving through it and he'll follow them around the same way.

That's a small, slightly melancholy thing about this life — the animals we sit with exist in continuity and we're the ones passing through. Buddy and Trixie will have other people they love after us. Gerald will have other humans he tolerates.

I've started taking better notes. It seems like the least I can do.
