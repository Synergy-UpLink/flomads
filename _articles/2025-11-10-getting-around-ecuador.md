Ecuador is a remarkably easy country to get around without a car, which surprised us given how mountainous it is. The intercity bus network is extensive, cheap, and generally reliable — the Andes routes are slow but scenic, and the coastal routes are faster than you'd expect. Here's what we learned over five months in and around Cuenca.

## Intercity buses

The main bus terminals (*terminales terrestres*) in Quito, Guayaquil, and Cuenca are proper hubs — large, organized, with dozens of companies running routes. Tickets are cheap: Cuenca to Quito (about 8 hours) runs around $12–15 depending on the company and seat class.

**Buy at the terminal, not online.** There are online booking platforms for some routes but they're inconsistently maintained and the terminal windows are fast and easy. Show up 30–45 minutes early for popular routes on weekends or holidays.

**Seat classes matter on overnight routes.** *Semi-cama* (reclining seats) and *cama* (full flat or near-flat) are worth the premium on anything over 4 hours. The *ejecutivo* seats on the better companies like Flota Imbabura and Transportes Ecuador are genuinely comfortable.

**Luggage goes under the bus.** You'll get a paper ticket for checked bags, which an attendant tears in half when you board. Keep your half. On busy routes, the cargo hold fills up — arrive early.

## Within cities

**Taxis** are inexpensive and the easiest option for most in-city trips. Cuenca has metered taxis; always confirm the meter is running or agree on a price before you get in. A ride across central Cuenca rarely exceeded $3.

**Ride apps** — InDriver works well in Quito and Guayaquil. In Cuenca we mostly used street taxis, which were plentiful enough that apps weren't necessary.

**The Cuenca tram** (*tranvía*) is worth knowing about — it runs a single east-west line through the historic center and is useful for getting between the bus terminal and the centro histórico. $0.30 per ride with a transit card, $0.35 cash.

## The Andes routes specifically

Getting between highland cities involves a lot of mountain roads. Cuenca to Loja, Cuenca to Riobamba, anything crossing the Cajas — these routes are beautiful and slow. Budget time generously. Fog, rain, and the occasional road closure for maintenance are all normal.

**Mountain sickness** is real above 3,000m. Cuenca sits at about 2,560m, Quito at 2,850m. Give yourself a day to adjust before doing anything strenuous. Coca tea is everywhere and the altitude headache remedy people swear by; we found it mildly useful and pleasant regardless.

## Getting to smaller towns

For towns off the main routes, *cooperativas* run smaller shared vans (*furgonetas* or *camionetas*) that leave when full. You find them at the main terminal or, in smaller cities, at a secondary van stop (*parada de camionetas*) that's often nearby but not always obvious. Ask someone at the terminal; Ecuadorians are generally helpful with directions.

## What doesn't work

**Hitchhiking** is common in rural areas and locals do it routinely, but as a foreigner you're likely to be charged van rates regardless. Not a problem, just a reset of expectations.

**Timing on Sunday** — service is reduced and some routes only run in the morning. If you need to travel Sunday afternoon, check the schedule the day before.

Overall: if you're comfortable with buses and willing to spend some time in terminals, getting around Ecuador independently is genuinely straightforward and inexpensive. We did the whole five months without once wishing we had a car.
